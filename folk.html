Folk music is a genre of music that has been passed down orally through generations within a particular culture or region. It often reflects the values, customs, and history of the people who created it. Here are some types of folk music:

Traditional Folk Music: This type of folk music refers to the songs and melodies that have been passed down through generations without any formal written record. Traditional folk music varies from country to country and region to region and reflects the culture and history of the people who created it.

Ballads: Ballads are a type of narrative folk song that tells a story through music. Ballads often have a simple melody and are sung in a storytelling style. They typically tell stories of love, tragedy, and historical events.

Protest Songs: Protest songs are a type of folk music that is used to raise awareness about social or political issues. They often have lyrics that express dissatisfaction with the status quo and a desire for change. Examples of protest songs include "Blowin' in the Wind" by Bob Dylan and "We Shall Overcome" by Pete Seeger.

Sea Shanties: Sea shanties are folk songs that were sung by sailors while they were working on ships. They were used to coordinate tasks and keep morale high during long voyages. Sea shanties often have a call-and-response structure and a simple melody that is easy to sing along to.

Regional Folk Music: Regional folk music refers to the folk music that is unique to a particular region or culture. Examples of regional folk music include Irish traditional music, Appalachian music, and Cajun music.

Folk Revival Music: Folk revival music is a type of folk music that gained popularity in the 1950s and 1960s. It was characterized by a renewed interest in traditional folk music and a desire to preserve it for future generations. Folk revival music was popularized by artists such as Joan Baez, Bob Dylan, and Pete Seeger.
